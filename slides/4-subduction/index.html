Overview

  • Subduction is a big part of global tectonics
  • Part of convenction in the mantle
  • Important global cycles: water and carbon
  • Understanding subduction is key to understanding the deep Earth

Image Subduction-en.svg from Wikimedia Commons by K. D. Schroeder, CC-BY-SA 4.0

Why does subduction happen?

Models predict that plates cool away from the ridge

  • As the plate cools, it gets denser
  • When the density is greater than that of the asthenosphere:
    • Gravitational instability
    • Plate sinks into asthenosphere
  • Sinking slab pulls the plate behind it (slab pull).

Imaging subducting slabs

  • Subducting slab heats and deforms causing deep earthquakes (70-300km)
  • This region is known as the Wadati-Benioff zone
  • Surrounding asthenosphere ductile and cannot cause earthquakes
  • Hypocenters give an image of the slab
  • Still used to determine slab geometry*

* For example, in the Slab2 model.

At the surface

Example from the Mariana Trench subduction.

  • Subducting plate bends under the load, creating trenches
  • Bending causes a forebulge next to the trench
  • Island arcs are often associated with the overlying plate

* In the example, the Pacific plate is older (hence cooler and denser). How can we tell from comparing the bathymetry the two plates?

At the subsurface

Image Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg from Wikimedia Commons by V. Ezekowitz, CC-BY-SA 3.0

  • Subduction brings water (in minerals) to the mantle
  • Hydration reduces the melting point of mantle rocks
  • Melt rises to the surface
  • Associated volcanism creates island arcs

Flexure of the lithosphere

Remembering from Lecture 2

Flexure of an elastic plate at equilibrium:

$ D\dfrac{\partial^4 w}{\partial x^4} = q(x) - (\rho_m - \rho_c) g w $ $D = \dfrac{E T_e^2}{12(1 - \nu^2)}$

$w$ = deflection, $q(x)$ = force density of load, $g$ = gravity,
$D$ = flexural rigidity, $E$ = Young's modulus,
$\nu$ = Poisson's ratio, $T_e$ = effective elastic thickness

Boundary conditions

Flexure under an end load:

  • No flexure away from the trench: $w \to 0$ as $x \to \infty$
  • Load $-V_0$ is applied at $x = 0$
  • The bending moment at $x= 0$ is $M_0$

Solution

  • Problem: In reality $M_0$ and $V_0$ are unknown.
  • Write solution as a function of $w_b$, $x_0$, and $x_b$.

$ w(x) = w_b\ e^{\pi/4} \sqrt{2} \exp\left[ -\dfrac{\pi}{4}\left(\dfrac{x - x_0}{x_b - x_0}\right) \right] \sin\left[\dfrac{\pi}{4}\left(\dfrac{x - x_0}{x_b - x_0}\right) \right] $

See "Geodynamics" section 3.17 for a derivation.

  • Convert bathymetry to flexure $w$ by subtracting a reference value.

Comparing to observations (continued)

  • Determine $x_0$, $x_b$, and $w_b$ from the graph.

Comparing to observations (continued)

  • Good agreement between model and observations.

Information about the lithosphere

  • Calculate flexural rigidity from $x_0$ and $x_b$:

    $D = \dfrac{64 (\rho_m - \rho_w) g}{\pi^4}(x_b - x_0)^4$

  • Calculate effective elastic thickness of the subducting lithosphere $T_e$ from:

    $D = \dfrac{E T_e^3}{12(1 - \nu^2)}$ | $Te \approx 28 km$

See "Geodynamics" section 3.16-17.

Gravity disturbances

Gravity and subduction zones

  • Large variation at subduction zones.
  • Characteristic positive-negative pair.
  • Andes, Caribbean, Japan, Mariana, Indonesia, etc.

Pacific plate subduction

  • Examine a profile of gravity disturbances and bathymetry cutting across Japan.
  • Include Sea of Japan (East Sea) and continental Asian for comparison.

Pacific plate subduction

  • Positive-negative pair at subduction.
  • Close to zero in most of the Pacific plate.
  • Slightly positive in Sea of Japan and Asia.
  • Small values are expected when we have isostatic equilibrium.

Modeling the subducting plate

  • Assume that the Eastern part is isostatically compensated.
  • Gravity disturbances of even layers that are compensated should be zero.
  • Assume signal is entirely due to flexure (forebulge and trench).

Modeling the subducting plate (continued)

  • Calculate the plate flexure model predictions.
  • What is the anomalous density distribution associated with the predicted flexure?

Density model for the disturbance

  • Gravity modelling is about stripping effects.
  • The disturbance is the observed gravity minus Normal gravity (from the ellipsoid).
  • Assume that the ellipsoid is layered (including an ocean).
  • The density anomalies are what exists (real) minus what was removed (normal).

Gravity disturbance predictions

  • Calculate model predictions using a Bouguer plate approximation: $g = 2\pi G \rho w$
  • $w$ is the plate flexure model predictions
  • For the forebulge: $\rho = \rho_c - \rho_w$ (top) and $\rho = \rho_m - \rho_c$ (bottom)
  • For the trench: $\rho = \rho_w - \rho_c$ (top) and $\rho = \rho_c - \rho_m$ (bottom)

Gravity disturbance predictions

  • Fit is reasonable for most of the plate.
  • The plate flexure model + our density model explains the forebulge and trench signals.
  • The model cannot fit small scale variations in bathymetry.

Foreland basins

Flexure on continental side

  • Flexure under the cordillera load should happen on both sides.
  • Gravity disturbance is negative in the foreland region as well.
  • Why don't we see a trench on the continental side?
  • Depressions tend to be filled by sediments.

Observations

  • Need to use basement depth instead of topography.
  • Estimates can come from seismology (but might not be very accurate).
  • Observed gravity disturbances are somewhat correlated with basement depth.

Flexure model

  • Treat basement depth as flexure ($w$).
  • Estimate $x_0$, $x_b$, $w_b$ for plate flexure model.
  • Effective elastic thickness: $Te \approx 118 km$
